Chapter 4

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

A disease that strips away voluntary movement while leaving the mind intact — and a scientific frontier full of urgent work.

Overview

What Is ALS?

✦

ALS — often called Lou Gehrig's disease — is a rapidly progressive neurodegenerative disease that attacks the motor neurons responsible for controlling voluntary muscle movement.

The name breaks down as: Amyotrophic (muscle wasting), Lateral (the sides of the spinal cord, where motor neuron pathways run), Sclerosis (scarring or hardening). Together these describe what happens in the disease: muscles shrink and weaken as the nerve signals that drive them are cut off.

ALS is less common than Alzheimer's or Parkinson's but progresses more rapidly. Most people with ALS live 2–5 years after diagnosis, though some, like physicist Stephen Hawking, live for decades. Cognitive function is usually preserved, meaning people are fully aware of what is happening to their bodies — a fact that makes the emotional burden of ALS particularly profound.

Brain and spinal cord changes

What Happens to Motor Neurons?

✦

ALS attacks two types of motor neurons:

Upper and Lower Motor Neuron Pathways

ALS damages both upper motor neurons (in the brain's motor cortex) and lower motor neurons (in the spinal cord). Without these signals, muscles cannot contract and gradually waste away.

⬆️ Upper Motor Neurons

These originate in the motor cortex of the brain and send signals down through the spinal cord to lower motor neurons. When they are damaged in ALS, muscles become stiff and spastic, and control of voluntary movement becomes impaired.

⬇️ Lower Motor Neurons

These are in the spinal cord (and brainstem) and send signals out to muscles. When lower motor neurons are lost in ALS, muscles no longer receive signals — they weaken, twitch involuntarily (fasciculations), and eventually waste away (atrophy).

Important distinction

ALS typically spares sensory neurons (touch, pain, temperature) and the neurons controlling eye movement, bladder, and bowel. This means most people with ALS retain their senses, bladder function, and the ability to move their eyes — even as other voluntary muscles are lost.

Disease course

Symptoms and Progression

✦

ALS can begin in different regions of the body — the arms, legs, or the muscles of speaking and swallowing (called "bulbar onset"). Regardless of where it starts, the disease typically spreads.

Early Stage

Symptoms often begin subtly — weakness in one hand or foot, slurred speech, or difficulty swallowing. Many people notice they are dropping things or tripping more often. Because symptoms can mimic other conditions, diagnosis is often delayed.

Middle Stage

Weakness spreads to other muscles. Mobility becomes significantly impaired; most people require assistive devices such as wheelchairs. Breathing can become affected as respiratory muscles weaken, requiring monitoring and eventually assisted ventilation.

Widespread muscle weaknessLoss of independent mobilityBreathing difficultiesSignificant speech changes

Late Stage

Most voluntary movement is lost. Communication may shift to eye-tracking technology or other assistive devices. Respiratory support (non-invasive or invasive ventilation) becomes essential. Cognitive function is typically preserved. End of life often results from respiratory failure.

Note on cognition: About 10–15% of ALS patients develop frontotemporal dementia (FTD). Research has shown ALS and FTD exist on a spectrum, with shared genetic and biological features — particularly involving a protein called TDP-43.

Care and research

Current Care and Research

✦

ALS management is multidisciplinary — a team of specialists works together because the disease affects so many body systems. No cure currently exists, but research is advancing.

💊 FDA-Approved Drugs

Riluzole (1995) was the first drug approved for ALS; it modestly extends survival by reducing glutamate toxicity. Edaravone (2017) reduces oxidative stress. Relyvrio (sodium phenylbutyrate/ursodoxicoltaurine, 2022) targets mitochondrial and endoplasmic reticulum stress — though its FDA approval status has been the subject of ongoing review.

🧬 Gene Therapy

About 20% of familial ALS cases are caused by mutations in the SOD1 gene. Tofersen, an antisense oligonucleotide drug approved in 2023, targets this mutation specifically — a landmark in personalized ALS treatment. Research is ongoing for other ALS-linked genes.

🔬 Stem Cell & Other Approaches

Researchers are exploring stem cell transplantation to support or replace lost motor neurons, as well as anti-inflammatory approaches targeting neuroinflammation in ALS. TDP-43 — the protein clumped in most ALS cases — is a major research target.

🤝 Palliative & Supportive Care

A specialized ALS care team typically includes neurologists, respiratory therapists, physical and occupational therapists, speech-language pathologists, nutritionists, social workers, and palliative care specialists — all working together to maximize quality of life at every stage.
