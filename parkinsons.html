Chapter 3

Parkinson's Disease

A disease of movement — and much more — caused by the loss of dopamine-producing neurons.

Overview

What Is Parkinson's Disease?

✦

Parkinson's disease is a progressive neurological disorder that primarily affects movement — but also impacts mood, cognition, sleep, and many other body systems.

It is the second most common neurodegenerative disease after Alzheimer's. Parkinson's develops when neurons in a specific part of the brain called the substantia nigra begin to die. These neurons produce dopamine — a chemical messenger critical for coordinating smooth, controlled movement. As dopamine levels fall, movement becomes harder to control.

Parkinson's disease was first described by English physician Dr. James Parkinson in 1817, who called it "the shaking palsy."

Brain changes

Dopamine and the Substantia Nigra

✦

Dopamine Pathway: Healthy vs. Parkinson's

In Parkinson's, dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra progressively die. Without sufficient dopamine, the striatum — which helps coordinate movement — cannot function properly. Lewy bodies (clumps of alpha-synuclein protein) form inside the affected neurons.

Lewy Bodies and Alpha-Synuclein

In Parkinson's disease, an abnormal protein called alpha-synuclein misfolds and clumps together inside neurons, forming structures called Lewy bodies. These accumulations interfere with normal cell function and eventually destroy the neurons that contain them.

The reason Parkinson's is so closely associated with movement problems is that the neurons most severely affected produce dopamine — the neurotransmitter that helps coordinate smooth, controlled movement. But dopamine also affects mood, motivation, sleep, and the gut, which is why Parkinson's has so many non-motor symptoms too.

Symptoms

What Are the Symptoms?

✦

Parkinson's symptoms are often divided into motor (movement-related) and non-motor symptoms. The four classic motor symptoms are sometimes called the "TRAP" of Parkinson's:

🤝 T — Tremor

An involuntary rhythmic shaking, most commonly in the hands or fingers at rest. The characteristic "pill-rolling" tremor — moving the thumb against the forefinger — is a classic sign.

💪 R — Rigidity

Muscle stiffness that can occur in any part of the body. Limbs may feel stiff and inflexible, making movement painful or difficult. Caregivers may feel resistance when moving a person's arm.

🐢 A — Akinesia / Bradykinesia

Slowness of movement (bradykinesia) or loss of spontaneous movement. Tasks that used to be automatic — like swinging arms while walking or blinking — become slow and effortful.

🏃 P — Postural Instability

Impaired balance and coordination that increases the risk of falls. The person may have a stooped posture and a shuffling walk. This usually develops in later stages.

Non-Motor Symptoms

These are often less recognized but can significantly impact quality of life:

Depression and anxiety Sleep disturbances (REM sleep disorder) Loss of smell (anosmia) Constipation Cognitive impairment Low blood pressure on standing Fatigue Soft speech (hypophonia) Swallowing difficulties

Did you know?

Many non-motor symptoms — like constipation, loss of smell, and sleep disturbances — can appear years before motor symptoms. Researchers are working to use these early signs to detect Parkinson's sooner.

Treatment

Treatment Options

✦

There is currently no cure for Parkinson's disease, but many effective treatments can significantly manage symptoms and maintain quality of life for years.

💊 Levodopa / Carbidopa

The gold standard treatment. The brain converts levodopa into dopamine, replenishing what has been lost. Carbidopa is added to make levodopa more effective and reduce side effects. Most effective for motor symptoms, especially early in disease.

🧲 Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)

A surgical procedure where electrodes are implanted in specific brain regions and connected to a pulse generator (like a pacemaker). Electrical stimulation helps regulate abnormal brain signals and can dramatically reduce tremors and other motor symptoms.

💉 Dopamine Agonists

Drugs that mimic the effect of dopamine in the brain. Often used in younger patients or in combination with levodopa. Examples include pramipexole and ropinirole.

🏃 Physical & Occupational Therapy

Exercise is one of the most powerful tools for managing Parkinson's. Programs like LSVT BIG (for movement) and LSVT LOUD (for voice) are specifically designed for Parkinson's. Dance and boxing programs have also shown benefits.
