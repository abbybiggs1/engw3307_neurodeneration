Chapter 1

What Is Neurodegeneration?

Understanding what happens inside the brain when nerve cells begin to break down.

The basics

How the Brain Works

✦

Before we can understand what goes wrong in neurodegenerative disease, it helps to understand what the brain normally does.

Your brain contains roughly 86 billion neurons — specialized cells that communicate with each other through electrical and chemical signals. These signals control everything you think, feel, sense, and do, from remembering a face to taking a step to breathing while you sleep.

Neurons connect to each other at junctions called synapses. When one neuron fires, it releases chemical messengers called neurotransmitters that travel across the synapse and activate the next neuron.

The central nervous system (CNS) — made up of the brain and spinal cord — is the command center of the body. Different regions of the brain handle different functions: memory, movement, language, emotion, and more.

The core concept

What Is Neurodegeneration?

✦

The prefix neuro- refers to neurons (nerve cells), and degeneration means progressive deterioration or loss. So neurodegeneration is exactly what it sounds like: a process in which neurons gradually lose their normal function and eventually die.

What makes this especially serious is that, unlike many other cells in your body, most neurons in the adult brain cannot be replaced. Once a neuron dies, its connections and the functions it supported are lost.

Key idea

Neurodegeneration is not a single disease — it is a category of diseases all characterized by the progressive loss of neurons. Different diseases target different types of neurons and different brain regions, which is why their symptoms vary so much.

The progression is usually slow. Symptoms often don't appear until a significant number of neurons have already been lost — sometimes 50–80% in the affected region. This is one reason these diseases are difficult to detect in early stages.

Root causes

Why Do Neurons Die?

✦

Scientists have identified several processes that contribute to neurodegeneration, though the exact triggers are still being studied.

🧩 Protein Misfolding

Proteins are molecules that perform critical jobs in cells. When proteins misfold — take on the wrong shape — they can no longer do their job and may clump together into toxic aggregates. This is a hallmark of Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease.

⚡ Mitochondrial Dysfunction

Mitochondria produce the energy neurons need to survive. When mitochondria malfunction, neurons are starved of energy and become vulnerable to damage and death.

🔥 Neuroinflammation

The brain's immune cells (called microglia) normally protect neurons. But in neurodegenerative disease, chronic inflammation can make things worse — damaging the very cells they are meant to protect.

🚛 Transport Failures

Neurons use a complex internal transport system to move nutrients and waste products. When this system breaks down, harmful materials accumulate and the neuron can no longer function properly.

Note: Most neurodegenerative diseases involve a combination of these mechanisms, and they interact with each other. Genetics, age, and environmental factors can all influence how and when these processes begin.
