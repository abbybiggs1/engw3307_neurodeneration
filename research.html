Resources & Glossary

Support organizations, further reading, and definitions for all key terms used in this guide.

Support organizations

Where to Find Help and Information

✦

Whether you are looking for support for yourself, a family member, or simply want to learn more, these organizations are trusted starting points.

Alzheimer's Disease

🧠
Alzheimer's Association — alz.org
The leading voluntary health organization in Alzheimer's care, support, and research. Includes a 24/7 helpline (1-800-272-3900), caregiver resources, and information on clinical trials.
 🏛️
National Institute on Aging (NIA) — nia.nih.gov
U.S. government resource with authoritative, science-based information about Alzheimer's and related dementias, clinical trials, and caregiver guides.
 🔬
Alzforum — alzforum.org
A research community hub covering the latest Alzheimer's and related dementia research news. More technical, but excellent for following recent scientific developments.

Parkinson's Disease

🔵
Parkinson's Foundation — parkinson.org
Dedicated to improving the lives of people with Parkinson's. Provides a helpline (1-800-473-4636), Parkinson's-specific exercise programs, and a network of Centers of Excellence.
 🦊
Michael J. Fox Foundation — michaeljfox.org
The world's largest private funder of Parkinson's research. Also hosts Fox Trial Finder, which helps connect patients with clinical trials.

ALS

⚡
ALS Association — als.org
The only national nonprofit organization fighting ALS on every front. Provides a helpline, local chapter support, equipment loan programs, and research funding information.
 🤝
Les Turner ALS Foundation — lesturnerals.org
A research and support organization with particular strength in ALS clinical care, family support services, and cutting-edge research programs.

General Brain Health & Research

🧪
ClinicalTrials.gov
The U.S. registry of all clinical trials. Search for ongoing Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, or ALS trials you may be eligible to participate in.
 🌍
World Health Organization — Dementia Fact Sheet
Global statistics, risk factor information, and WHO initiatives on dementia and brain health.

Glossary

Key Terms Defined

✦
Neuron

A specialized nerve cell that transmits electrical and chemical signals throughout the nervous system. The basic functional unit of the brain.

Central Nervous System (CNS)

The brain and spinal cord together. The command center that receives, processes, and sends signals throughout the body.

Synapse

The tiny gap between two neurons where chemical signals (neurotransmitters) are passed from one cell to the next.

Neurotransmitter

A chemical messenger that carries signals across synapses. Examples include dopamine, serotonin, glutamate, and acetylcholine.

Neurodegeneration

The progressive loss of structure or function of neurons, including their death. The underlying process in diseases like Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and ALS.

Cognitive Decline

A decrease in mental abilities such as memory, language, reasoning, and attention that is greater than expected with normal aging.

Protein Misfolding

When a protein takes on an abnormal three-dimensional shape that prevents it from functioning properly. Misfolded proteins often clump together and become toxic to cells.

Neuroinflammation

Inflammation within the brain or spinal cord. While it can be protective initially, chronic neuroinflammation contributes to neuronal damage in many neurodegenerative diseases.

Hippocampus

A seahorse-shaped brain region critical for forming new memories and spatial navigation. One of the first regions damaged in Alzheimer's disease.

Microglia

The immune cells of the central nervous system. They protect the brain from damage and clear cellular debris, but can contribute to harm if chronically activated.

Mitochondria

Organelles inside cells that produce energy. Often called the "powerhouses of the cell." Dysfunction in mitochondria is linked to neurodegeneration.

Amyloid Plaque

A sticky deposit of beta-amyloid protein fragments that accumulates between neurons in the brains of people with Alzheimer's disease, disrupting cell communication.

Tau Tangle (Neurofibrillary Tangle)

Twisted fibers of the tau protein that build up inside neurons in Alzheimer's disease, collapsing the internal transport system and causing cells to die.

Dementia

An umbrella term for a group of symptoms — including memory loss, confusion, and impaired judgment — severe enough to interfere with daily life. Alzheimer's is the most common cause.

Beta-Amyloid (Aβ)

A protein fragment that normally exists in the brain but in Alzheimer's disease accumulates into plaques. Its exact role in causing neuronal damage is still being researched.

Cholinesterase Inhibitor

A class of drugs (e.g., donepezil, rivastigmine) that slow the breakdown of acetylcholine in the brain, temporarily improving memory and thinking in Alzheimer's patients.

APOE ε4

A variant of the APOE gene that is the strongest known genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's disease. Having one copy increases risk; having two copies increases it further.

Dopamine

A neurotransmitter critical for coordinating smooth movement, motivation, and reward. Its depletion in the substantia nigra is the primary cause of Parkinson's motor symptoms.

Substantia Nigra

A small region deep in the brain (part of the basal ganglia) that produces dopamine. In Parkinson's disease, the neurons of the substantia nigra progressively die.

Lewy Body

An abnormal clump of alpha-synuclein protein that forms inside neurons in Parkinson's disease and some related conditions. Named after Frederich Lewy who first described them.

Alpha-Synuclein (α-syn)

A protein normally found throughout the brain. In Parkinson's disease it misfolds and aggregates into Lewy bodies, damaging and eventually killing the neurons that contain them.

Bradykinesia

Slowness of movement, one of the cardinal symptoms of Parkinson's. Tasks that used to be automatic — walking, blinking, writing — become slow and effortful.

Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)

A surgical procedure in which electrodes are placed in specific brain regions and connected to a pulse generator. Electrical stimulation reduces tremor and other motor symptoms of Parkinson's.

Levodopa

The most effective medication for Parkinson's. The brain converts levodopa into dopamine, replenishing what has been lost. Usually combined with carbidopa to improve effectiveness.

Motor Neuron

A neuron that carries signals from the brain or spinal cord to muscles, enabling voluntary movement. ALS specifically destroys motor neurons.

Upper Motor Neuron

Motor neurons originating in the brain's motor cortex that send signals down through the spinal cord. Damage causes spasticity and weakness.

Lower Motor Neuron

Motor neurons in the spinal cord and brainstem that send signals directly to muscles. Damage causes muscle weakness, twitching (fasciculations), and wasting (atrophy).

TDP-43

A protein involved in gene regulation. In ~97% of ALS cases, TDP-43 misfolds and forms aggregates inside neurons. It is also implicated in frontotemporal dementia.

Fasciculations

Involuntary muscle twitches visible under the skin, caused by dying lower motor neurons firing spontaneously. A characteristic early sign of ALS.

Riluzole

The first FDA-approved drug for ALS (1995). It modestly extends survival by reducing glutamate toxicity — the overstimulation of neurons by the neurotransmitter glutamate.

Antisense Oligonucleotide (ASO)

A type of gene-silencing therapy that uses short DNA-like sequences to block the production of harmful proteins. Tofersen is an ASO drug approved for SOD1-ALS.

Frontotemporal Dementia (FTD)

A type of dementia primarily affecting personality, behavior, and language. Occurs in 10–15% of ALS cases; ALS and FTD share overlapping biology including TDP-43 pathology.
