Alzheimer's Disease

The most common form of dementia — what it does to the brain, what it feels like, and where research stands.

Overview

What Is Alzheimer's Disease?

✦

Alzheimer's disease is a progressive brain disorder that slowly destroys memory and thinking skills, and eventually the ability to carry out even simple tasks.

It is the most common cause of dementia, accounting for an estimated 60–80% of all dementia cases. In the United States alone, more than 6 million people are living with Alzheimer's disease, most of them aged 65 and older.

Alzheimer's is not a normal part of aging, though the greatest known risk factor is advancing age. The disease is named after Dr. Alois Alzheimer, who first described it in 1906 after observing unusual changes in the brain tissue of a patient who had died after years of severe memory problems.

Brain changes

What Happens in the Brain?

✦

Two abnormal structures are the hallmarks of Alzheimer's disease at the cellular level: amyloid plaques and tau tangles. These develop years — sometimes decades — before symptoms appear.

Amyloid Plaques & Tau Tangles

Amyloid plaques form in the spaces between neurons. Tau tangles form inside neurons, collapsing the internal scaffold that keeps them healthy.

Amyloid Plaques

Amyloid plaques are clumps of a protein fragment called beta-amyloid that accumulate in the spaces between neurons. In a healthy brain, these protein fragments are broken down and cleared away. In Alzheimer's, they build up and cluster together, interfering with communication between neurons.

Tau Tangles

Inside healthy neurons, a protein called tau helps stabilize internal structures called microtubules — imagine them as train tracks that transport nutrients and other materials through the neuron. In Alzheimer's, tau proteins change shape and clump together into twisted "tangles," collapsing the tracks and causing the neuron to malfunction and die.

Where does it start?

Alzheimer's typically begins in the hippocampus — the brain region most involved in forming new memories — which is why short-term memory loss is usually one of the first symptoms. As the disease progresses, it spreads to other regions of the brain.

What to expect

Symptoms and Progression

✦

Alzheimer's disease progresses in stages. Most clinicians describe a mild, moderate, and severe stage, though the timeline and specific symptoms vary greatly between individuals.

Early Stage (Mild)

People may appear largely independent but notice lapses in memory — especially for recent events — or have trouble finding words. They may repeat questions or misplace familiar objects. Many people in this stage continue to work and participate in social activities.

Middle Stage (Moderate)

This is typically the longest stage. Memory loss and confusion grow significantly. People may struggle to recognize family members, need help with daily activities like dressing or bathing, and experience changes in personality or behavior such as wandering, suspicion, or agitation.

Late Stage (Severe)

In the final stage, people lose the ability to communicate verbally, recognize loved ones, or care for themselves. The brain can no longer control basic bodily functions. Full-time care is required. Infections, especially pneumonia, are a common cause of death.

Treatment

Current Treatments and Research

✦

There is currently no cure for Alzheimer's disease, but several treatments can manage symptoms and, more recently, slow disease progression.

💊 Symptom-Managing Medications

Cholinesterase inhibitors (such as donepezil) boost levels of a chemical messenger involved in memory and judgment. Memantine regulates another messenger to slow symptom progression. These do not stop the disease but can improve quality of life.

🧬 Anti-Amyloid Therapies

A new class of drugs called anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies — including lecanemab (Leqembi) and donanemab — target amyloid plaques directly and have shown they can slow cognitive decline in early-stage disease. Approved by the FDA in 2023–2024.

🧠 Lifestyle Approaches

Research supports that regular physical activity, mental engagement, social connection, quality sleep, and heart-healthy diet may lower risk and support brain health — though they cannot halt the disease once it has started.

🔬 Research Frontiers

Scientists are exploring early detection through blood tests, tau-targeting therapies, inflammation-reducing drugs, and gene therapy. The goal: catch the disease before symptoms appear and intervene early.
